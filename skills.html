FAQ

A list of skills and processes is a start, but to help any potential employers out there (and my mother) gain more insight into my UX prowess, I've answered some frequently asked questions.

What is UX design? Why does it matter?

I believe that design overall is the creation of products or services (or stories!) that enhance the human experience. User experience design focuses on solving the problems of a specific user or user group and encompasses all user-facing aspects of a system. Empathy, pragmatism and creativity are applied to researching and validating solutions — a process that leads to building intuitive and delightful products or services (or stories!)

Why it matters. When a product or solution is created with a user-centered foundation to solve specific user problems, not only will it be intuitive and delightful, but it will resonate more deeply with users, thus driving engagement, retention and growth.

What is the difference between UX and other design disciplines?

A great product experience starts with UX design to create the foundation (the bones) of a product, which encompasses the entire user journey and the architecture of the product. Only then should the process move on to UI design (the life support systems), which encompasess all user interactions and touchpoints with the product. Often, UI design also encompasses the visual design (the cosmetics) of the product. Though, just as often this can be a separate function.

Over the course of my career, I have been responsible for all three disciplines. In one role. And as much as I love beautiful things, my passion lies in providing exceptional, easy-to-use experiences over beautiful ones. <nerd moment>Like Tron, I fight for the user.</nerd moment>

What is your design process?

In a perfect world, my UX process is as follows:

  1. Define the problem to be solved and determine the business goals (ask questions, questions and more questions)
  2. Perform user research to determine user needs and challenges as it relates to the problem
  3. Diagram user flows and wireframes to shape possible solutions to the problem. Collaborate and gather feedback with stakeholders, and preferrably users, to arrive at a final solution
  4. In conjunction with wireframes, develop content/content strategy and information architecture. Determine key performance indicators and success metrics
  5. Finalize wireframes by designing the user interface for the solution
  6. Design high-fidelity mockups and a prototype for continued collaboaration and usability tests
  7. Support engineering by answering questions as they crop up, provide feedback and iteration options during sprints. Perform user acceptance testing
  8. Launch solution and pop the bubbly!
  9. Measure and track the performace of the solution against the business goals and established key performance indicators
  10. Recommend and champion ideas for improvement for next iteration of the solution

What’s your process for working with developers, product managers and other designers?

I have always held roles that place me in a position to translate to engineering what the business needs to accomplish and when (yesterday!), and then communicate back to the business what engineering can accomplish in a palatable timeframe. I help non-techies understand the technology stack and techies understand the value of marketing.

One of my superpowers is to take the big ideas that the business dreams up and create realistic, fully executable solutions. Solutions, which if vetted to solve user challenges, can be easily broken down into epics and users stories.

Working with other designers is a pleasure for me. I consider myself an excellent team player and motivator and enjoy the brainstorming and sharing of ideas that push a solution to the next level. There's nothing better than a room full of designers and a fresh pack of post-it notes.
